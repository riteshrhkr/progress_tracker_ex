== SECTION 1: Question Types Classification

Understanding the *type* of question being asked is the first step. Each type demands a different mental approach.

=== Technical Question Types

[cols="1,2,3", options="header"]
|===
| Type | Description | Example Trigger Words

| **Definition**
| What is X?
| "What is...", "Define...", "Explain..."

| **Comparison**
| X vs Y, differences/similarities
| "Difference between...", "Compare...", "When to use X over Y"

| **Internal Working**
| How does X work under the hood?
| "How does...", "What happens when...", "Walk me through..."

| **Scenario-Based**
| Given a real situation, what would you do?
| "Suppose...", "If you had to...", "Imagine..."

| **Problem Solving**
| Solve a code/design problem
| "Design a system...", "Write code for..."

| **Debugging / Troubleshooting**
| Find the bug or fix the issue
| "Why is this failing?", "What's wrong here?"

| **Opinion / Trade-off**
| Your view on approaches or technologies
| "Which would you prefer?", "What are the trade-offs?"
|===

=== Non-Technical Question Types

[cols="1,2,3", options="header"]
|===
| Type | Description | Example Trigger Words

| **Behavioral**
| Past experience / how you handled situations
| "Tell me about a time...", "Give me an example..."

| **Situational**
| Hypothetical future scenarios
| "What would you do if...", "How would you handle..."

| **Motivational**
| Why you chose this path/company/role
| "Why do you want...", "What motivates you..."

| **Self-Assessment**
| Strengths, weaknesses, self-awareness
| "What are your strengths?", "Where do you need improvement?"

| **Introduction / Summary**
| Overview of who you are
| "Tell me about yourself"

| **Conflict / Teamwork**
| Interpersonal dynamics
| "How do you handle disagreement?", "Tell me about a conflict..."
|===

== SECTION 2: Thinking Process for Each Question Type

The goal: *slow down your brain, identify the type, then follow a mental script.*

=== 2.1 Definition Questions

[source,text]
----
Step 1: Identify the concept being asked
Step 2: Recall the core definition (1-2 lines)
Step 3: Think of ONE supporting property or characteristic
Step 4: Recall ONE real example or use case
Step 5: Speak: Definition → Why it matters → Example
----

> **Mental cue:** "What is it? → What does it do? → Where is it used?"



=== 2.2 Comparison Questions

[source,text]
----
Step 1: Identify the two (or more) things being compared
Step 2: Recall the PRIMARY difference (the most important one)
Step 3: Recall 1-2 secondary differences
Step 4: Think of use cases: "When would I pick X over Y?"
Step 5: Speak: Difference → Use case for each → Recommendation
----

> **Mental cue:** "Same family, different purpose."

=== 2.3 Internal Working Questions

[source,text]
----
Step 1: Identify the system/component being asked about
Step 2: Think about it in phases: Input → Process → Output
Step 3: Walk through the lifecycle or flow step-by-step
Step 4: Add any important edge behavior (e.g., thread safety, exceptions)
Step 5: Speak in sequence — avoid jumping back and forth
----

> **Mental cue:** "Trace the flow from start to end."

=== 2.4 Scenario-Based Questions

[source,text]
----
Step 1: Clarify the scenario in your head (or ask 1 quick clarifying question)
Step 2: Think: "What's the problem? What's the goal?"
Step 3: Think through your approach (steps or options)
Step 4: State trade-offs if applicable
Step 5: Speak: Situation understanding → My approach → Result/Reasoning
----

> **Mental cue:** "Problem → Solution → Why it works."

=== 2.5 Behavioral Questions

[source,text]
----
Step 1: Identify the theme: leadership, conflict, failure, achievement?
Step 2: Pick a specific real story from memory
Step 3: Map it to STAR: Situation → Task → Action → Result
Step 4: Focus most on Action (what YOU did)
Step 5: Close with learning or impact
----

> **Mental cue:** "STAR — and keep Action the longest part."

=== 2.6 Comparison / Opinion / Trade-off Questions

[source,text]
----
Step 1: Accept that there is no single right answer
Step 2: State your lean (which you prefer or recommend)
Step 3: Give 2 reasons — pros of your choice, cons of the other
Step 4: Add: "It depends on context..." if needed
Step 5: Speak with confidence, not with hedging
----

> **Mental cue:** "Take a side, justify it, acknowledge the other."

== SECTION 3: Answer Structure

=== Structures by Question Type

[cols="1,2", options="header"]
|===
| Question Type | Answer Structure

| **Definition**
| Definition → Key Property → Example

| **Comparison**
| Core Difference → Use Case X → Use Case Y → Recommendation

| **Internal Working**
| Overview (1 line) → Step-by-Step Flow → Edge Case / Note

| **Scenario-Based**
| Understand the problem → Approach → Justification → Result

| **Behavioral**
| Situation → Task → Action (focus here) → Result → Learning

| **Self-Assessment**
| State clearly → Brief explanation → Real example

| **Motivational**
| Short reason → Connect to role/company → What you'll bring
|===

=== Universal Answer Rule

[NOTE]
====
**Always open with a direct answer, then explain.**

❌ Wrong: "So, uh, basically HashMap is... you know, it stores key-value pairs and... the hash function..."

✅ Right: "HashMap is a key-value data structure. It uses a hash function to determine storage location. For example, in a user cache, we use it to retrieve user objects by ID in O(1) time."
====

== SECTION 4: Knowledge Recall Strategy

=== 4.1 The Keyword Anchor Technique

When you blank out on an answer:

[source,text]
----
1. Repeat the question silently in your head
2. Find ONE keyword in the question (e.g., "transaction", "bean", "scope")
3. Free-associate: what do you know about that keyword?
4. Start with that — a partial answer is better than silence
----

=== 4.2 Chunking for Memory

Group related concepts into chunks to prevent overload:

[cols="1,2", options="header"]
|===
| Chunk Group | Examples

| Spring Core
| IoC, DI, Bean lifecycle, ApplicationContext

| Spring Boot
| Auto-configuration, starters, embedded server, actuator

| Java Collections
| List, Set, Map — then sub-types per interface

| Java Concurrency
| Thread, Runnable, synchronized, ExecutorService, CompletableFuture

| Behavioral Themes
| Leadership, Conflict, Failure, Achievement, Teamwork
|===

=== 4.3 Start Early, Not Late

[IMPORTANT]
====
Begin your answer *before* you have it fully formed. +
Starting breaks the mental freeze. The rest follows naturally.

Say: "So, [concept] is primarily used for..." — your brain catches up as you speak.
====

=== 4.4 The "3-Point Rule"

When unsure how much to say:

> Aim for exactly **3 points**. It feels complete without rambling.

- Point 1: Core idea
- Point 2: How it works or why it matters
- Point 3: Example or comparison

== SECTION 5: Communication & Clarity Rules

=== 5.1 The Opening Rule

Always begin with a **direct statement**, not a build-up.

[cols="1,1", options="header"]
|===
| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Do This

| "So basically, if we look at it from multiple angles..."
| "The key difference is..."

| "It's a bit complicated but..."
| "Simply put, X does Y."

| "I think maybe..."
| "In my experience..."
|===

=== 5.2 Sentence Length

- Use **short sentences** (8–12 words) for clarity.
- End each thought before starting the next.
- Pause briefly between ideas — silence is confidence, not weakness.

=== 5.3 Avoid Filler Words

Common fillers to eliminate:

> "um", "uh", "you know", "basically", "kind of", "sort of", "like"

**Fix:** Replace with a **brief pause**. Silence sounds more confident than filler.

=== 5.4 Pacing

[source,text]
----
- Speak at 70% of your natural speed
- Pause after each key point (1 second)
- When nervous: SLOW DOWN, not speed up
- Use pauses to signal a transition: "Now, the second point is..."
----

=== 5.5 When You Don't Know

[source,text]
----
Say: "I haven't used that specific feature, but based on my understanding of [related concept], I'd approach it by..."
----

> Never say "I don't know" and stop. Always bridge with what you DO know.

== SECTION 6: Common Mistakes

=== Technical Questions

[cols="2,2", options="header"]
|===
| Mistake | Fix

| Jumping into details before defining the concept
| Always start with a 1-line definition

| Using jargon without explanation
| Briefly define any term that might be unclear

| Explaining everything you know (brain dump)
| Stop at 3 points; offer to go deeper

| Saying "it depends" without saying what it depends on
| State the condition: "It depends on whether you need X or Y"

| Giving a wrong answer confidently
| If uncertain, say: "I believe it's X, but I'd verify before using it in production"
|===

=== Behavioral Questions

[cols="2,2", options="header"]
|===
| Mistake | Fix

| Giving a vague or generic answer ("I'm a team player")
| Use a specific story with named people and measurable results

| Speaking about "we" instead of "I"
| Focus on YOUR actions: "I decided...", "I escalated...", "I proposed..."

| Skipping the Result in STAR
| Always end with the outcome — numbers, decisions, or feedback received

| Picking a story that makes you look bad overall
| Frame even failures as learning moments with a positive close

| Over-explaining the Situation
| Keep Situation brief (2-3 sentences). Most time should go to Action.
|===

=== Self-Assessment / HR Questions

[cols="2,2", options="header"]
|===
| Mistake | Fix

| Giving fake weaknesses ("I work too hard")
| Give a REAL weakness with an active mitigation plan

| Listing too many strengths without evidence
| Pick 2 strengths with one example each

| Rambling on "Tell me about yourself"
| Use a 90-second structure: Current → Past → Why here
|===

== **_SECTION 7: Examples with Full Breakdown_**

=== 7.1 Technical: Definition Type

==== Example 1: What is Dependency Injection?

*Question:* "What is Dependency Injection in Spring?"

*Thought Process (internal):*
[source,text]
----
- Type: Definition
- Keyword: "Dependency Injection"
- Core idea: Objects don't create their own dependencies; they receive them
- Supporting point: Promotes loose coupling, testability
- Example: Service class injecting a Repository
----

*Final Answer:*
[quote]
____
Dependency Injection is a design pattern where an object's dependencies are provided externally rather than created by the object itself. In Spring, this is achieved through `@Autowired` or constructor injection, where the IoC container manages the object lifecycle. For example, a `UserService` doesn't create a `UserRepository` — Spring injects it, making the code testable and loosely coupled.
____

---

==== Example 2: What is a Spring Bean?

*Question:* "What is a Spring Bean?"

*Thought Process (internal):*
[source,text]
----
- Type: Definition
- A bean = an object managed by Spring's IoC container
- Lifecycle: created, configured, used, destroyed
- Example: any class annotated with @Component, @Service, @Repository
----

*Final Answer:*
[quote]
____
A Spring Bean is any object that is instantiated, assembled, and managed by the Spring IoC container. You define beans using annotations like `@Component`, `@Service`, or `@Bean` in a config class. The container controls their lifecycle — creation, dependency injection, and destruction — freeing the developer from manual object management.
____

---

=== 7.2 Technical: Comparison Type

==== Example 1: HashMap vs ConcurrentHashMap

*Question:* "What is the difference between HashMap and ConcurrentHashMap?"

*Thought Process (internal):*
[source,text]
----
- Type: Comparison
- Primary difference: Thread safety
- HashMap: not thread-safe, faster in single-thread
- ConcurrentHashMap: thread-safe, uses segment-level locking (Java 7) / CAS (Java 8+)
- Use case: multi-threaded environments → ConcurrentHashMap
----

*Final Answer:*
[quote]
____
`HashMap` is not thread-safe — concurrent modification can cause data corruption or infinite loops. `ConcurrentHashMap` is designed for concurrency; in Java 8+, it uses CAS operations for high-throughput thread-safe access. Use `HashMap` in single-threaded contexts for better performance, and `ConcurrentHashMap` in multi-threaded environments like caches or shared state management.
____

---

==== Example 2: @Component vs @Bean

*Question:* "When would you use `@Component` vs `@Bean`?"

*Thought Process (internal):*
[source,text]
----
- Type: Comparison
- @Component: auto-detection via classpath scanning, used ON the class
- @Bean: manual registration in a @Configuration class, used ON a method
- Use @Bean when you don't control the source class (e.g., third-party libraries)
----

*Final Answer:*
[quote]
____
`@Component` is used when you own the class and want Spring to auto-detect it through classpath scanning. `@Bean` is used in a `@Configuration` class when you need explicit control over object creation — especially for third-party classes you can't annotate. For example, you'd use `@Bean` to register a Jackson `ObjectMapper` with a custom configuration.
____

---

=== 7.3 Technical: Internal Working Type

==== Example 1: How does Spring's @Transactional work?

*Question:* "Walk me through how `@Transactional` works internally in Spring."

*Thought Process (internal):*
[source,text]
----
- Type: Internal Working
- Flow: proxy creation → method call interception → begin tx → execute → commit/rollback
- Key: Spring creates a proxy around the bean
- Important detail: self-invocation won't trigger transaction (same class call)
----

*Final Answer:*
[quote]
____
When Spring detects `@Transactional`, it wraps the bean in a proxy (JDK or CGLIB). When a transactional method is called, the proxy intercepts the call, begins a database transaction, delegates to the real method, then commits on success or rolls back on an unchecked exception. One key gotcha: calling a `@Transactional` method from within the same class bypasses the proxy, so the transaction won't activate.
____

---

==== Example 2: How does HashMap work internally?

*Question:* "How does `HashMap` work internally in Java?"

*Thought Process (internal):*
[source,text]
----
- Type: Internal Working
- Flow: put() → hash key → find bucket → store entry (linked list / tree in Java 8+)
- Key: hashCode() + equals() determine bucket and uniqueness
- Edge: collision handling, load factor, resize
----

*Final Answer:*
[quote]
____
`HashMap` stores key-value pairs in an array of buckets. On `put()`, it computes `hashCode()` of the key to find the bucket index. If two keys land in the same bucket (collision), they're stored as a linked list (or a red-black tree in Java 8+ when size > 8). On `get()`, it recomputes the hash and uses `equals()` to find the exact entry. When the map exceeds its load factor (default 0.75), it resizes to double the capacity.
____

=== 7.4 Non-Technical: Introduction

==== Example: "Tell me about yourself"

*Thought Process (internal):*
[source,text]
----
- Type: Introduction / Summary
- Structure: Current role → Past experience → Why this role
- Keep it 60-90 seconds
- End with enthusiasm for this opportunity
----

*Final Answer:*
[quote]
____
I'm a Java/Spring developer with 4 years of experience building backend services. Currently, I'm working at [Company], where I've built REST APIs and improved system performance by 30% through query optimization. Before that, I completed a strong foundation in data structures and system design. I'm looking to grow into a role with more architectural ownership, and based on what I've read about your product, this team aligns really well with that goal.
____

---

=== 7.5 Non-Technical: Strengths & Weaknesses

==== Example: "What is your biggest weakness?"

*Thought Process (internal):*
[source,text]
----
- Type: Self-Assessment
- Pick a REAL weakness that is not a core job skill
- Pair it with an active fix — shows self-awareness and maturity
- Keep it concise: Weakness → Impact I noticed → What I'm doing about it
----

*Final Answer:*
[quote]
____
I tend to over-engineer solutions — I sometimes build more flexibility into a system than the immediate problem requires. I noticed this was slowing down delivery, so I've started applying the YAGNI principle and doing quick alignment checks with stakeholders before going deep on implementation. It's helped me ship faster without sacrificing quality.
____

---

=== 7.6 Non-Technical: Conflict Handling

==== Example: "Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a team member."

*Thought Process (internal):*
[source,text]
----
- Type: Behavioral (STAR)
- Theme: Conflict → resolution → outcome
- Focus on YOUR actions, not blame
- End positively: relationship preserved or lesson learned
----

*Final Answer:*
[quote]
____
**Situation:** During a sprint, a colleague and I disagreed on whether to use a microservice or a monolithic approach for a new feature. +
**Task:** We had a deadline and needed a decision. +
**Action:** I scheduled a 30-minute sync, laid out both approaches with trade-offs, and proposed we go with the monolith to meet the deadline, with a documented plan to extract it later. +
**Result:** We shipped on time, the tech lead appreciated the pragmatism, and we followed through on the extraction in the next quarter.
____

== SECTION 9: Practice Drills

=== 9.1 Daily 10-Minute Drill (Technical)

[source,text]
----
Day 1-2: Pick 5 definition questions. Answer each in exactly 3 sentences.
Day 3-4: Pick 3 comparison questions. Draw a mental table before answering.
Day 5-6: Pick 2 internal-working questions. Narrate the flow like a story.
Day 7: Full mock — mix 1 technical + 1 behavioral + 1 HR question. Time yourself.
----

=== 9.2 Daily 5-Minute Drill (Behavioral)

[source,text]
----
Write down 1 story from your experience per day.
Map it to STAR format on paper.
Read it aloud once.
Trim it to under 90 seconds.
----

=== 9.3 The "One Concept, Three Ways" Drill

Pick any technical concept. Explain it three ways:

. **To a junior developer** — simple language, analogy
. **To a peer developer** — technical, precise
. **To a non-technical manager** — business impact, no jargon

> This drill builds true understanding — you can't explain what you don't know.

=== 9.4 The "30-Second Rule" Drill

[source,text]
----
Take any concept.
Set a 30-second timer.
Explain it clearly before the timer ends.
If you can't — you don't know it well enough. Study it.
If you can — you're interview-ready on that topic.
----

=== 9.5 Mock Interview Simulation

[cols="1,3", options="header"]
|===
| Round | Practice Set

| **Round 1** (10 min)
| 3 technical questions: 1 definition, 1 comparison, 1 internal working

| **Round 2** (5 min)
| 1 scenario question + 1 opinion/trade-off question

| **Round 3** (5 min)
| "Tell me about yourself" + 1 behavioral + 1 self-assessment

| **Debrief**
| Write what you said, what was unclear, and what to improve
|===

=== 9.6 Weekly Review Habit

[source,text]
----
Every Sunday:
  - List 5 concepts you struggled to explain this week
  - Re-read/re-study them using chunking
  - Practice their answers using the 30-second rule
  - Add 1 new behavioral story to your bank
----
